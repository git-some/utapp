Green Roof Experimentation and Application

In an effort to reduce energy usage and rain water runoff from a building, UCF Landscape and Natural Resources has teamed up with the Sustainability and Energy Management Department on campus to create and maintain a native plant species green roof. The departments use high tech monitoring systems that record and evaluate the data being collected on the roof to show that the building is actually saving money and reducing untreated rain water run-off.

Restoration (Alafaya Frontage Native Shrub Seeding)

In an effort to restore a diverse plant species community, Landscape and Natural Resources performs a yearly, native grass and shrub seeding. By spreading native shrub and grass seed among the understory of a restoration site, other nonnative plant species will have a harder time at successfully establishing on restored area, which can further promote these native plant species and eventually help increase the native plant and animal biodiversity, around the UCF community.

Map - Mediterranean Landscape

A temperate to sub-tropical biome, vegetation types range from forests to woodlands, savannas, shrublands, and grasslands. "Mosaic" landscapes are common. Much of the woody vegetation in Mediterranean-climate regions is sclerophyll, which means 'hard-leaved' in Greek. Mediterranean eco-regions are semi-arid, and often have poor soils, so they are vulnerable to degradation by human land-use. These regions are also some of the most endangered on the planet, and many eco-regions have suffered tremendous degradation and habitat loss through logging, overgrazing, conversion to agriculture, urbanization, and introduction of exotic species. Many native plants and animals have become extinct or endangered with in these systems. Many of the plants are pyrophytes, or fire-loving, adapted or even depending on fire for reproduction.

Flora: Herbs (Rosmarinus officinalis, Origanum vulgare, Thymus vulgaris), Fig Tree (Ficus carica), Olive (Olea europaea, Bucida buceras), Grape (Vitis), Dwarf Pomegranate (Punica granatum nana).

Map - Cypress Dome

Forested wetlands (swamps) that are dominated by bald cypress and/or pond cypress are called cypress domes. Generally these are shallow circular depression wetlands with a vaulted canopy because smaller trees grow in the shallower waters at the outer edge with the taller, older trees growing in the center. Pond cypress trees usually dominates these dome swamps in pine flatwoods with mucky, organic soils. Swamp gum, red maple, sweetbay (magnolia), dahoon holly, swamp bay and slash pine may also be found in the canopy.

Fauna: Moccasin, Swallow Tail Kite, Pileated Woodpecker, Cottonmouth, Salamander.

Map - Sago Palms Landscape

This is an eclectic display of plants that are planted purely for horticultural aesthetics.There is no central theme to the origin of the plants. Golden Dewdrop is native to the Americas, from Mexico and the West Indies south to Argentina in South America. Its showy blossoms attract butterflies and hummingbirds. Though Queen Sago resembles a palm it is actually a cycad. Historically, the pulpy stem has been used to make a type of flour; however the Queen Sago has also been known to cause adverse neurological affects and paralysis. It is native to equatorial Africa.

Flora: Golden Dewdrop (Duranta erecta), Queen Sago (Cycas Circinalis).

Map - Xeric Landscape

Xeric shrub-lands are a biome characterizes by a dry climate with an average winter rainfall of 10 inches or less a year. Deserts and other hyper-arid ecosystems characterize these climates. The specimen in Xeric landscapes consist mostly of the Neartic desert climate region of northwestern Mexico. Plants adapted to these types of environments are called xerophytes, and include succulents, geophytes, sclerophyllous (leathery-leaved) perennial and annual plants.

Flora: Agave (Agavoideae Agave), Giant Mexican Sunflower (Tithonia diversifolia), Prickly Pear Cactus (Opuntia).

Map - Bonsai House

Bonsai (Japanese: literally "potted plant") is the art of miniaturization of trees by restraining their growth to an aesthetic ideal. Cultivation includes techniques for pruning, wiring, grafting, defoliation, deadwood, leaf trimming, watering, and repotting in various styles of containers.

'Bonsai' is a Japanese pronunciation of the earlier Chinese term penzai. The word bonsai is used in the West as an umbrella term for all miniature trees, but traditionally should be applied only to container-grown trees following the Japanese tradition.

Map - Skunk Vine

The UCF's cypress dome, at the corner of Gemini Boulevard and North Orion Boulevard is a lush, five-acre wetland plagued with an invasive species named the skunk vine. The vines are of a non-native predatory plant that, if left unchecked, will strangle the native cypress and other plant life out of its habitat. Since the construction of Gemini Blvd, the cypress dome has been overrun with the vine, a problem that has needed constant attention. It is similar to kudzu, a vine in the Appalachian Mountains, which can sweep literally every square inch of the side of a mountain, from the forest floor to the tops of trees. They report more than 10 invasive species on campus, ranging from small, isolated incidents to full-blown, widespread infestations such as the skunk vine, and they anticipate having their hands full well into the future.

Read the Central Florida Future article for more information about invasive skunk vine removal efforts: http://www.centralfloridafuture.com/volunteers-weed-out-invasive-vine-1.1760472

Map - Solar PV

In 2009, UCF installed two photovoltaic solar arrays on the Engineering buildings. An 11 kW array is on the side of Engineering 1 and a 21 kW array on the roof of the Harris Engineering building. The Engineering 1 array provides enough energy to power two residential homes.

Map - Solar Charge Station

Students, faculty, and staff members can charge their electric vehicles at this plug-in carport on the east side of Memory Mall. The charging station uses solar panels to produce electrical energy. Any unused energy generated is re-routed to UCF's power grid.

Map - Solar Hot Water

On the roof of the Towers 2 residential hall, UCF installed a solar thermal system, which heats water with the sun's energy. This system provides hot water for the 500 residents living in the building, reduces the need for electricity, and reduces greenhouse gas emissions.

Map - Thermal Energy Storage

The TES facility allows UCF to produce chilled water during the nighttime hours, while the lower electric rate is in effect, and then store the chilled water in the tank for cooling the campus at a future time. By capitalizing on these off peak rates, the tank produces close to $700,000 in savings for the University and helps the utility company manage its peak load.

Map - Harris Corps

In 2009, UCF installed two photovoltaic solar arrays on the Engineering buildings. An 11 kW array is on the side of Engineering 1 and a 21 kW array on the roof of the Harris Engineering building. To put this into perspective, the Engineering 1 array provides enough energy to power two residential homes!

Map - Green Roof

High atop the Student Union rests a garden that soaks in rainwater, beautifies UCF's campus and saves energy costs. As part of a research study by the UCF Stormwater Management Academy and the state Department of Environmental Protection, the green roof that crowns the building provides a visual and environmental appeal. UCF hopes to set a trend for other universities and businesses to take on environmentally responsible practices such as green roofs, which create both economic and social benefits while protecting valued natural resources. While the green roof clearly reduces heating and cooling expenses, pinpointing the amount of savings is difficult because heating, ventilation and air conditioning expenses cannot easily be separated for sections of the addition covered by the two types of roofs. If the experiment continues to go well and the community embraces the idea, the university will consider installing green roofs on other buildings and creating one that allows public access.

Map - Physical Sciences

The Physical Sciences building was UCF's first LEED Gold building, which means it was built with environmental considerations. It has a green roof with native plants, energy efficient lighting, water efficient fixtures, and Florida friendly landscaping just to name a few!

About

This map app, named Knight's Earth, was inspired by the Unifying Theme of UCF focused on the environmental and global climate change:Our Planet. Our Education. Our Future. It became apparent how necessary it is to connect the everyday person with UCF's energy conservation, sustainability, and environmental research, initiatives, and facilities on campus. This app will allow you to access most up to date and location specific information available at your fingertips by putting the information in your hands if you are walking around campus with your mobile device or allowing remote exploration through the map web site. In collaboration with UCF Departments such as Sustainability & Energy Management, faculty, staff, and student organizations we were able to compile the latest statistics and photographs which enhances your UCF experience. New, exciting developments in our environmental programs and easy ways that students can make a positive impact on their environment are easily lost when the information is hard to find. However modern technology has the power to transform small hand-held smart devices and websites into interactive learning tools that can be used to guide yourself around UCF's campus and to greater environmental awareness.

We hope our research will equip you, the everyday user and visitor with relevant environmental knowledge enhanced with a visual map. Similar technology and dedicated interdisciplinary research can help us to manage and monitor our environmental impact at a large metropolitan university such as UCF. This in turn will reduce the risks associated with waste, global climate change, air and water quality, chemical health hazards that plague our environment and our daily lives. This will highlight UCF's commitment to becoming a leader in energy and environmental sustainability, thus bringing together the UCF community centered on our pledge to be climate-neutral by 2050. Go Knights!

The rooftop solar panels have successfully maintained the vehicle charge to date. ZimRide

UCF's Zimride community is a simple way to find friends and colleagues who are going the same way you are. Zimride is a private community that makes it easy to share the seats in your car or catch a ride.

It's simple. Have a car? Split the costs by driving fellow UCF community members. Don't have a car? Sign up, post your ride and Zimride does the matching! You save money, reduce traffic, protect the environment and have more fun when you ride!

Membership is free! All UCF staff, faculty, and students with a UCF email are eligible to sign-up for our private Zimride Rideshare Community here: http://zimride.ucf.edu/
